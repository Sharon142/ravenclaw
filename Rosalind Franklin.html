Rosalind Franklin


Summary


Rosalind Elsie Franklin was born on July 25 1920 in London, England. Rosalind Franklin
died on April 16, 1958 London. She was a British scientist and a chemist known for her
discovery of the molecular structure of DNA. Franklin also gave a new insight to the
structure of viruses which helped to build foundation of the virology structure. Franklin attended
St Paul's Girls' College before pursuing her studies in physical chemistry at the University of
Cambridge. Franklin graduated in 1941 and received a companionship to research in physical chemistry.
The following year, Franklin became an air-raid warden due to World War II and used this opportunity to
investigate the physical chemistry between carbon and coal. Franklin joined the Biophysical Laboratory in
King's College 1951 to conduct a research study on DNA using X-ray diffraction methods. This successfully
helped her discover the structure of DNA.


Discovery of DNA and the structure of viruses


Brenda Maddow referred to Rosalind Franklin as “The Dark Lady of DNA” due to the lack of recognition
she received. Franklin moved to Paris in 1946 to perfect her skills in X-ray crystallography. She loved
the freedom and lifestyle in Paris but she moved back to London to accept her job at King’s College. During
her life, Rosalind Franklin was hardworking and a bold traveler who loves the outdoors and hiking. Franklin
enjoys a vibrant discussion on science and politics. Rosalind Franklin’s friends and colleagues considered
her as a kindhearted, brilliant, short-tempered, and stubborn woman. Maurice Wilkins was one of her colleagues
who considered her a challenge to work with.


A misunderstanding occurred between Rosalind Franklin and Maurice Wilkins, causing immediate friction and making
them work in isolated environments. It was unknown to Rosalind Franklin that Maurice Wilkins showed James Watson
and Francis Crick Franklin’s work. They never acknowledged Franklin’s work, only after Rosalind Franklin’s death
Crick acknowledged her contributions. In his letter, Crick has stated that Rosalind Franklin’s contributions had
been vital for the discovery of DNA and their Noble Prize award.


Around this time Rosalind Franklin left King’s College for the Birkbeck College crystallography laboratory. Franklin
studied the structure of the tobacco mosaic virus where she became successful and was asked to create virus models to
display at the 1958 Brussels World’s Fair Science Exhibition. Franklin died at 38 due to ovarian cancer (few years
before Wilkins, Watson, and Crick received the Noble Prize Award). The inscription on her tombstone states: “Her work on
viruses was a lasting benefit to mankind”. Her reputation and her legacy grew and spread. This creates a rise in the
feminist movement and a strong response to James Watson’s portrayal which is a memoir called “The Double Helix”.
Despite missing out on an award, Rosalind Franklin’s name is remembered through awards, science institutes, and a university
in Chicago. Franklin’s legacy will continue to extend in space where a space rover in Mars will be named after her.
